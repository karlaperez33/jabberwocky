Alice on the Jab Walls 

Mitchel Resnick, who graduated from SU in 1976, painted the Jabberwocky murals on the club's sound-proof tectum walls. The murals portray scenes from Lewis Carroll's "Alice in Wonderland" and provided a backdrop for the second stage location from 1979 to 1985. After the demolition of Kimmel Dining Hall, some of the mural panels were moved to the Newhouse School and WAER radio station, while others remain in storage.

Playing the Jab

The Jab was also a spot for local musicians to develop a following. Gary Frenay is a Syracuse-based singer-songwriter who founded two bands that regularly played at the Jab—the Flashcubes (1977) and Screen Test (1980). According to Frenay, the club's student-based crowd helped launch his punk, new wave band, the Flashcubes, since students were more familiar—than the general demographic in Syracuse—with the developing NYC scene, which included artists like Patti Smith and Blondie. Frenay described the Jab's atmosphere as always dark, with a lingering smell of beer. Still, the small-sized club created an intimate setting where the artists had to walk through the crowd to get to the dressing rooms, meaning audience members could directly interact with their favorite musicians.

"It was a very cool thing; we played gigs, and they'd be jumping around like crazy, jumping on the stage at the end of the gig. That was how the band happened, really, it was because of Jab," Frenay said.

The Last Hurrah

In 1984, a college student's worst nightmare became a reality—the drinking age in the U.S. was raised from 18 to 21. According to Rezak and Frenay, the National Minimum Drinking Age Act signified the Jab's death as three-fourths of the student population were now not allowed to drink on campus—thus, live music at SU moved underground. 

But, the iconic club would not go easily. In 1985, using money from Miller Beer sponsorships, organizers booked a week's worth of funeral festivities to close out Jabberwocky, starting off with blues artists John Hammond and Junior Wells. The rest of the week featured Roger McGuinn from the Byrds, Jorma Kaukonen, John Cale from the Velvet Underground, and "The Hardest Working Man in Show Business" James Brown. Surprisingly, Brown played Thursday night instead of the closing night on Friday, but his show was sold out with standing room only. With Brown's signature splits and capes up on that tiny stage, the Jab got its last hurrah.

A Forgotten Piece of Campus History

More than 55 years after its debut, Jabberwocky is no more than a memory—and rubble. As students walk by the Kimmel Hall demolition site, nothing lingers but the inevitable sense of oblivion. This project aims to use an emerging media technology to immortalize a forgotten piece of campus history. A special thanks to Gary Frenay for sharing his story and archival photos!
